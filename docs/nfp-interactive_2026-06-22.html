What is the "NFP vs JOLTS Gap"?

JOLTS measures the actual flow of workers: Hires − Total Separations = net change in employment. NFP measures the same concept via a separate payroll survey. They should roughly agree. When NFP prints consistently above JOLTS implied net, it signals the Birth-Death model is adding phantom jobs — and benchmark revisions tend to follow. When NFP is below JOLTS, it tends to get revised up. This gap has predicted the direction of the last three benchmark revisions.

2026 Gap = +20k/month — Largest since 2024

In 2025 (post-benchmark), the gap had compressed to just +6k/month — nearly clean. In 2026 YTD the gap has re-widened to +20k/month. This is consistent with the B-D model re-adding phantom spring hiring in L&H and PBS, the same sectors that were revised away in the 2024 and 2025 benchmarks. The 2026 gap isn't extreme, but the direction is the wrong one — and it's tracking the early 2024 pattern.

What would change the thesis (Bull case)

The BLS modified the B-D ARIMA model starting Jan 2026 — incorporating live sample data into the seasonal adjustment. If that fix works, the gap is smaller than it appears. JOLTS job openings have also ticked UP (7,618k Apr 2026 vs 6,550k Dec 2025), suggesting genuine demand recovery in some sectors. A hires rate move above 3.6% or quits above 2.3% would confirm real tightening, not B-D noise.

Market Skepticism — Is NFP Trusted?

Short answer: No — trust has materially declined since 2024

Three consecutive benchmark revisions (2023 upward, 2024 and 2025 downward) and a survey response rate collapse from 59% → 43% have caused mainstream economists and the Fed itself to discount headline prints. The "beat/miss" reaction to NFP releases has structurally weakened — markets now wait for revisions and JOLTS corroboration before trading the number with conviction.

BLS Survey Response Rate: 59% (2019) → 43% (2025)

This is the least discussed but most important structural factor. When 57% of establishments don't respond, the model-based imputation (Birth-Death) fills the gap. A lower response rate means MORE B-D model weight — exactly when the model's seasonal assumptions are most likely to be wrong. The preliminary benchmark announced in Sep 2025 was −911k. The Feb 2026 final benchmark was −1.03M cumulative over 2024–2025.

Benchmark Revision Timeline

  • Aug 2024
    BLS preliminary estimate: −818k jobs
    Pre-announcement of March 2024 benchmark revision. First major public signal that 2023–2024 prints were inflated. Caused significant market repricing in Sep Fed expectations.
  • Feb 2025
    Final 2024 benchmark: −598k jobs (March 2024 baseline)
    Final revision came in smaller than the Aug preliminary, but still removed 598k jobs. Private sector bore most of the cut. L&H and PBS confirmed as primary sources of overstatement.
  • Sep 2025
    Preliminary 2025 benchmark: −911k jobs (March 2025 baseline)
    BLS announced a second consecutive large downward revision — even larger than 2024. 880k private sector. Confirmed the B-D model overstated 2024–2025 hiring systematically. Secretary of Labor issued an unusual public statement.
  • Jan 2026
    BLS modifies Birth-Death ARIMA model
    For the first time since the model was introduced, BLS incorporated current sample information into the ARIMA seasonal adjustment component. Applied retroactively to Apr–Dec 2025 monthly estimates. Intended to reduce the systematic spring overstatement.
  • Feb 2026
    Final 2025 benchmark: −1.03M cumulative over 2024–2025
    BLS recalculated the full 2024–2025 series. Two-year job growth revised dramatically lower. 2024 initial +1,324k (Jan–May) → +751k revised. 2025 initial +838k (Jan–May) → +182k revised. Formally the weakest post-COVID labor market since the pandemic reopening ended.
  • Sep 2026
    Next preliminary benchmark (March 2026 baseline) — WATCH
    Resolution event for 2026 YTD. If B-D modification is working, 2026 gap should compress vs 2024. If preliminary shows >−400k, the model's spring phantom is still active despite the methodology change. Philly Fed Q3 early benchmark will provide a signal ~2–3 months prior.

Why the B-D Model Overstates in Spring

1. Net Business Formation Assumption

B-D assumes that new businesses formed each month are creating jobs at a historically stable rate. During periods of declining business confidence, the model assumes formation that isn't happening — especially in L&H (restaurants, hotels) and PBS (staffing, consulting).

2. Spring Seasonality Bias

March–May is when B-D adds the most phantom jobs — construction season, hospitality hiring season. When demand is soft, the seasonal template overestimates. In 2024 and 2025, the spring B-D contribution was not offset by higher survey response rates, so the overstatement went undetected until the full QCEW data arrived.

3. Survey Non-Response Amplifies the Problem

Non-responding establishments are assumed to behave like respondents. In 2025, with 57% non-response, this assumption covered a massive share of the labor market. The QCEW (the authoritative tax record) later revealed these non-respondents had lower employment — hence the revision.

Wages & Job Quality — Is Earning Power Rising?

More jobs, but the composition is shifting toward lower-wage sectors

Average Hourly Earnings (AHE) growth is decelerating from 5.1% (2022) toward 3.6% (2026 est). But the aggregate masks a composition problem: the sectors gaining jobs in 2026 (L&H at ~$21/hr, Ed&Health at ~$31/hr) are not the same as those losing them (Information at ~$56/hr, Financial at ~$48/hr). A healthcare aide added in 2026 ≠ a software engineer lost in 2023.

The composition effect: nominal AHE can look healthy while marginal job quality declines

If L&H adds 118K jobs at $21/hr while Information sheds 59K at $56/hr, the aggregate wage bill math still shows positive growth — but the new jobs are lower quality. In 2022, the labor market added 2,108K jobs broadly across all wage tiers. In 2026, +569K is concentrated in the lowest-wage tier and institutional healthcare. The marginal worker added in 2026 has materially lower earning power than the average worker added in 2022.
